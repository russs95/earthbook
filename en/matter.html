Three hundred million years ago, Earth’s oceans were full of swirls of spiralling matter swiming merrily about. Just as fish are ubiquitous in our oceans today, for millions of years the ancient ammonite swam abundantly in the seas of the Mezoic. Famous for its spiral fossilized shell, the now extinct amonite had much in common with its Moluskan descendants today. Like the clams and oysters that we know well, the ammonite sieved loose particles of carbon and calcium out of the water to synthesis its shell. And just like the mollusks of today, its defecations, shell and corpose would inevitably float down to the ocean floor. Over time, the relentless rain of expired ammonites, accompanied by the carcasses of other marine creatures and their excretions, accumulated. As layer upon layer of sediment pressed down upon another, steadily, all the carbon and calcium that each ammonite had gathered was compacted into stone. Over millions of years, the lives of trillions of marine creatues such as the amonite, spun countless tons of bicarbonate into geological sequestration. Today, in its fossil form we can hold an ammoniate's ecological contribution in our hand. In helping to add to the vast limestone deposits of today, over time, the ammonite's relentless subtraction of carbon helped reduced the net acidity of the oceans, making the waters more hospitable homes for countless other creatures to thrive.





THE WAY IN WHICH THE ANCIENT AMMONITE managed it matter to build its own home and steadily enrich the common home that it shared with other creatures, is an example that can guide and inspire us today. In our last chapter, we saw how the outward spin of energy is characteristic of Earth’s pattern. Now, looking closely at the life cycles of the ammonite and modern mollusks we can discern the geometry of Earth's spin of matter. As we shall see by looking closely at the amonite and other Earthen organisms, over the last billion years they have tended to inexorably spin their principal elements towards concentration and sequestration. This tendency forms our third Earthen ethic. With its help we can deepen our appreciation of the ecological enrichment wrought by the way the Igorots managed their defacations and residuals. More importantly, by understanding their example, we gain a new tool to evaluate our modern management of matter and our modern goals of "net-zero" and "zero-waste". With the Earth's example as a guide, we can be sure the way our processes, products and systems manage their carbon and other elements are truly are green.

To begin, let us retrun one more time to Earth's primordial origins for a closer look.

As we saw in Earth's stellar story, our solar system's planets formed five billion years ago as interstellar debris steadily swirled together. This coalescence of matter into planet Earth is in fact a process that has never ceased.

On our planet, as on all others, meteors continue to fall; geology, atmosphere and oceans all continue to spin and spiral– ever churned by the ever arriving blaze of the sun.⁷⁷

However, on each and every planet, the way that matter comes together and spirals is unique.

On every planet, vortexes unfold with their own unique geometric character. Determined by a planet's unique configuration (its orbit, spin, chemistry, magnetosphere, mass, momentum and more). From Venus sulfuric acid dipole vortexes, to Jupiters' polygonal vortex patterns of hydrogen and helium, to Saturn's hexagonal hydrogen polar vortex, the way each planet spirals its matter is a direct reflection of its cosmological character-- in particular the swirl of its primary elements.

On Venus, Jupiter and Saturn, the prevalence of certain principal elements— sulfur, hydrogen and helium— dominated and determinined the character of their spirals. On Earth, the spin of carbon, oxygen and hydrongen has determined ours.

As Earthen systems strove to dissipate the Sun's ever arriving shine, carbon came together into more and more complex systems. Steadily, as early cellular systems unfurled, they figured out how to gather the abundant loose carbon from the atmosphere to build their bodies. As early organisms evolved, their own intricate patterns of carbon began to unfurl.

Like the ammonite!

But also, just like trees, fish and fungi. Steadily, as generations of mollusks, plants, fungi animals and insects lived and died, their biomass, whether on land or sea, was covered up by the fall of the next.

Although these organisms all added CO2 back into their environment through their exhalations and excretions, with each cycle of life and death, they tended to concentrate more carbon than they let out loose.  Be it on the floor of an ocean or of a forest, slowly and steadily, layers of life were buried one upon the other.  Over hundreds of millions of years, the inexorable accumulation of these ancient organisms became compacted deep underground. In the case of the ammonite, as more and more of their carbonate shells, bodies and excretions fell downwards, it meant that more and more carbon was being effectively subtracted from the water's themselves. This in turn enabled the ocean's to absorb more carbon dioxide from the atmosphere above.

With the life cycles of innumerable organisms systematically subtracting more carbon than they added, soon vast quantities were stored out of the atmosphere and into geological storage. With so much carbon sequestered into geological storage, its ocean's reduced in acidity and the atmospheric carbon content declined.

While its impossible to determine today, just how much carbon the ammonites themselves were responsible for, studies of modern mollusks show that the potential is significant. In the case of the forests of the carboniferous period, 100 million years before, the net substraction was dramatic. The subtraction of carbon from the cycles of plants, trees, forests and algae was so substantial that it lead to million of year ice ages. Despite such extremes however, this pattern of steady subtraction served to counter balance inevitable planetary moments of release– such as forest fires, volcanoes and meteor strikes. In this way, over the course of the planet's last 600 million years, as trillions of carbon-based organisms lived and died, on the whole the planet's climate tended steadily towards stabilization.

And even more life was able to flourish in abundance and diversity.

This dynamic of matter management can be found in kincentric cultures and cyclocentric nations around the world.(see our definition of the term cyclocentic in Chapter 5)

As we saw earlier, the lives of the Igorot people of Northern Luzon are guided by their ayyew ecological ethos. This ethos guided the way that the Igorots managed the matter that passed through their hands and bodies. From farming, to eating; from discarding, to defecating, the way in the Igorots embodied ayyew in their process resulted not in "waste" (their is no such concept in the Igorot language) but in "logits" (or, roughly translated "fertilizer").

Each Igorot household home had a lomeng— a submerged enclosure where the family pig was kept. However, these were much more than pig stalls. Lomeng were used to discard everthing from overcooked rice, grass clippings. They were also used as a toilet. The pigs would consume almost everything given to them! They would grow fat and provide rich manure to fertilize the families gardens and fields— essentially concentrating the various forms of carbon biomass given to them into even more concentrated and nutrient rich biomass.

Over, countless lifecycles of humans and pigs, this process lead to the steadily concentration of carbon, nitogen and phosphos— the prinpal elements of their ecosystem— back into the soil. This lead to ever more fertile fields and healthy and well nourished Igorot communities.

As we strive to ensure our human enterprises are green, the ways of Igorots, the Ammonites and of the Earth show us the way forward.

Just as the Earth tended it’s cycles towards the inward spiral of matter, so must we intend and achieve the same with our own. Only when the intention and the result of our cycles is the net concentration of their active elements, are we following the pattern of our planet. Only then is the third Earthen ethic met. Only then can our acts and enterprises be considered ecological contributions— and green.

This third earthen ethic provides a geometric means of evaluating the management of matter within our human processes. It applies to the principal elements used in any of our processes— but most notably to our usage of carbon.

Perhaps the most tangible example of our modern use of carbon, is of plastic. As we saw in Plastic's Stellar story, plastic is a by-product of the refinement of petroleum to make fuels. As we send our used plastic off to far off factories to be processed, dumped or incinerated, our industrial processes begin again with new material. Having originated from a concentrated form under the earth to be dispersed over the surface of the planet, we can clearly observe that the outwards spin of plastic industrial cycles is contrary to the inward spiral of the Earth's.

What then are we to make of modern attempts at green— such goals as "net-zero" and "zero-waste"?

In so far as these goals seek to reduce and balance the outputs of our process to zero, they are a step in the right direction. However, from an Earthen perspective we can see that they their geometry is lacking.

As we saw in the example of the Igorots, every molecule of matter in the processes of the Igorots, once its nutrient energy had been extracted, was returned back into the cycles of life around in them in a denser form than before. With the rice plants extracting loose carbon from the air, condensing it in to rice, and Igorot cultivation continuing the downward spiral into further concentration, steadily more and more carbon was sequestered into biomass and into the soil itself.

As we develop enterprises, technologies and industries to be green, the requisite geometry by which they must manage their matter is clear.

Whether the process’s primary element is carbon or calcium, lithium or silicone, the process must embody the third Earthen Ethic.

First, the process must accumulate its principal elements. Second, the cycle of the process must concentrate these elements. Third, the spiral of these cycles must then lead to the net-subtraction of the element and tend, over the long-term, to its sequestration.

Circular, for-Earth processes that can achieve this, embody both the inward spiral of their matter and the outward spiral of their energy and fulfill the first three Earthen ethics.

In so doing, the tension between their distribution of energy and their concentration of matter, unfurls through space and time.

Like those cycles of the Igorot, Salmon, Ammonite and the Earth.

In sharp contrast to the desolation that unfolded on the surfaces our fellow planets, the geometry of this Earthen double spiral has led to just the opposite:

A never ending spin towards ever more vibrant and diverse systems.

Our fourth Earthen ethic.
