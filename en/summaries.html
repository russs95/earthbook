BEFORE A TREE BECOMES A TREE, its entirety is summed up in its seeds. In this spirit, we’ve put together a short sentence, a long sentence and a paragraph to define and describe the theory we will be laying out in the chapters ahead.

Let’s start with a short, one sentence definition of Earthen ethics:

That which follows the Earth’s example is green.

Now, here’s a longer sentence that defines green from an Earthen perspective. This one includes “must” to make it an ethic:

To be a green ecological contribution a process must follow the Earth’s pattern of concentrating matter and dispersing energy to enrich the vibrancy, diversity and consciousness of the system of which it is a part.

Now, let’s put our ethic within the broader context that we’ll be exploring:

Certain societies have mastered the art of ecological integration far better than others. Rather than deplete the ecosystems of which they were a part, they steadily enriched them. Our modern civilization has been doing the just opposite. The pattern of our modern processes— including those we aspire to be green (in fact, especially these ones!)— is systematically ecologically depleting. Like our plastic. Derived as it is from fossil-fuels, plastic holds within it our modern story and Earth’s story. By retracing plastic’s full multi-billion year story, we can see the characteristic pattern by which our planet greened the biosphere: the way in which Earth’s spiraled its matter and energy inwards and outwards to the benefit of all. Through this dual spiral, Earth’s systems unfurled into ever more vital and diverse systems that grew more consciousness of their interconnection. Looking at ecological enriching cultures we can see this same pattern in their ecological integration. To follow their example, we must first amend our metaphysics to add human ecological contribution to our imagination. What we call ‘green’. Then, to be green our processes must likewise embody Earth’s pattern in managing their energy and matter. To do so, our enterprises must account for their ecological impacts and show that they have indeed achieved a net-dispersal of energy, a net-concentration of matter and a vitalization of the diversity and consciousness of the ecosystems that they are a part of. Following the Earth’s example we can return to our plastic and embody the pattern of contribution into its management. This way we can transition our plastic management (and our other processes) from depletion to contribution— from grey to green.

Yes, that’s a tad technical! However, our seeds sown, we now know where our roots are headed and how our branches will unfurl.

So let’s begin.
