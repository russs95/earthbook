The Medium is the Message.
DO YOU like to have a glimpse of a book's background before you begin? Or perhaps you appreciate a quick summary before jump in? Well, either way, you're in luck.

In the culture of the Igorot people of Northern Luzon that inspires this particular book, story telling is central. In the stone dap'ay plazas at center of every village, stories are told to introduce people, lessons and ideas. It is an example, that we will follow in the chapters of the Tractatus Ayyew ahead.

To start, Banayan and I will share the story of how this book was begun— the unlikely events and collaboration that it represents. For the eager reader, we then provide a short sentence, a long sentence and a paragraph that summarize the theory we'll be laying out in the rest of the book.

Of course, how a story is told (or a theory for that matter) is just as important as where it is told. In the dap'ay, stories were best told around a fire, with the listeners gathered round close.

Alas, while our book can't recreate that particular atmosphere, we've nonetheless put great care into its presentation and publication. After all, the way an idea is expressed says just as much about it as the words it contains. In this way, the Tractatus Ayyew is published first as an Earthbook— an Earth and reader friendly format that is hand coded from the ground up to embody the principles that we'll be developing in the Tractatus Ayyew.

The culmination of a decade of dialogue and reflection that Banayan and I have shared, the Tractatus Ayyew represents our culminating conviction that when it comes to the way we do things (from publishing a book, to managing an economy), the wisdom traditions of ancient indigenous cultures¹ (like the Igorots) are of critical modern relevance.

Our title reflects this intersection of worlds. In Latin, the word tractatus refers to the philosophical tradition of a treatise— a systematic exposition of a particular concept.  In Kan’kan’ue, the word ayyew refers to the virtue of cyclic ecological integration that guides the culture of the Igorot people. 

It is this ayyew ethos, and our experience of it, which undergirds the theory of green and grey presented in the three books of the Tractatus Ayyew—   what we call an Earthen ethics.

In Book One we clear the ground and establish the foundation of our Earth based ethics.

In Book Two we dedicate a chapter to each of the five Earthen ethics. 

In Book Three we conclude with our five-fold solution for the management of plastic that can be applied to other green-aspiring enterprises.

In this way, the Tractatus Ayyew truly aims to be more a tool kit than a manifesto.

While the Tractatus progresses sequentially, each chapter is written as a standalone essay that represents one facet Earthen ethics. This way, each chapter’s argument may be readily referenced to address particular fallacies, issues and arguments. Within the Earthbook format, each chapter has its own page, that can be easily shared. For example, this particular section can be found at https://book.earthen.io/preamble.html

To represent the modular conceptual geometry of the Tractatus Ayyew, each chapter’s first page begins with a mandalic representation of its place within the whole.
