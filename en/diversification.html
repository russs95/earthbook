In the sunny shallows of oceans around the world, coral polyps, tiny marine invertebrates, build their home. Working with even tinier sun-powered zooxanthellae plankton that live within them, the duo capture carbon and calcium out of the waters around them. Living in colonies of thousands individuals, each polyp uses the nutrients that it collects to secrete a calcium carbonate exoskeleton around itself. As one polyp dies and another takes its place, each generation builds upon the skeletons of the last. Steadily, cycle by cycle their home-building fratally unfolds. Domes, shelves, horns, fans and a myriad of varying perturbations blossom. The vast surface areas of these geometric palaces provides a three dimensional neighbourhood of niches, shelters and microhabitats. As many species of fish, invertebrates, and microorganisms make their home within the space, their cycles of life intertwine. Complex and interconnected relationships emerge. This web of dependencies and interactions vitalizes the co-evolution of diverse strategies and adaptions to exploit or evade these relationships. Cycle by cycle, as the tiny polyps and their colony flourish, so too does the vitality, resiliency and diversity of the ecosystem of which they are a part.





The process by which the coral polyp builds its home, and in so doing creates a common home for a great diversity of others, is an example to inspire and guide our green aspirations. Indeed, the polyp's process of spiraling its matter into sequestration and distributing energy out to its ecosystem, perfectly embodies our last two Earthen ethics. While, in our last two chapters we looked individually each ethic of energy and of matter, in fact these two are never seperate. When both an organism's spirals are in Earthen resonance, our fourth Earthen ethic unfolds. Through its s confluence of spirals, the coral's blossoming of surface area and three-dimensonal space unfolds. As we shall see, Earth's unfolding over the last billion years, parralels the polyp's process, making clear our fourth Earthen ethic of diversification. By looking at the way the Earth transformed from barren to biodiverse, and by noting the way in which scientists have measured this transition, the parameters of the principle become clear. In particular, in the correlation between a system, its space and diversification. With the help of the fourth Earthen ethic we can further make sense of the ecological enrichment wrought by cyclocentric cultures and, we gain a new tool to evaluate our modern enterprises to be sure they truly are green.

To begin, let us return one more time to Earth's primordial beginnings for a closer look.

As we saw in Earth's stellar story, over the course of the last four billion years, the cosmological character of our planet has unfurled from barren to biosphere. Despite cataclysms and extinction a singular upward tendency prevailed. The way in which energy and matter have swirled together unfolded through inxorable accumulation of material habitats and through mutuaslic energetic dependencies and interactions which have vitalize ecosystems to become more resilient, abundant and diverse.

Nowhere in the fossil record is the process of diversification more evident than in the oceans.

In 1998, paleontologist Joseph Sepkoski completed a comprehensive database of marine life over the last 541 million years. The database compiled over 30,000 fossil genera, representing the finds and categorization of millions of species. By recording each genus’s appearance and disappearance in the fossil timeline, he was able to chart for how long each were active and when they died off.⁷⁷

Sepkoski was particularly interested in showing the Earth’s great rises and falls in diversity — such as the explosion of life in the Carboniferous to the rise and fall of the dinosaurs. Indeed, his timeline of extinctions correlates directly with the dating of great meteors and volcanoes by geologists. However, just as important as showing declines in diversity, his curve rises — demonstrating clearly that over Earth's history, the diversity of the marine biosphere steadily increased.(See figure 1)

The underlying mechanism by which this biodiversity unfurled on Earth are parraled by the example of the coral polyp.

the process of making space to support others, which is exemplified by the coral polyp, is a microcosm of the broader process that has shaped Earth's diverse and vital biosphere over billions of years.

First, just as the homes of coral polyps accumulate over time to provide the foundation for the entire reef ecosystem, so too has the flourishing of life on Earth resulted in the gradual accumulation of living organisms and their impacts on the planet. The emergence of photosynthetic cyanobacteria, for instance, began to transform Earth's atmosphere by releasing oxygen, making it more habitable for other life forms. Over billions of years, the contributions of various organisms, from plants to animals, fungi, and microorganisms, have collectively shaped the planet's ecosystems so that other organisms could find a home and flourish themselves. Similar to the diversity of species within a coral reef, the process of evolution has generated a vast diversity of species, each contributing in their own way to the common home of all.

Secondly, the success of coral reefs is dependent on mutualistic relationships between corals and the symbiotic algae that provide them with energy. Like the biodiversity within coral reefs, Earth's biosphere has thrived due to the intricate web of life forms that support and depend on each other. In a broader sense, Earth's transformation also relies on intricate ecological relationships. The coevolution of species and their interactions, such as pollination, seed dispersal, predation, and competition, has led to the development of complex and mutually beneficial despersions of nutrients. These ecological relationships have allowed various species to occupy specific niches and adapt to changing environmental conditions, ensuring the planet's long-term biodiversity and ecological stability. This interdependence ensures the sustainability and resilience of ecosystems, as well as the continued evolution of life on the planet.

Such a support for diversity is also reflected in the ecological integration of cyclocentric cultures.

As we saw in our first two Earthen ethics, the Igorots and the Wetsuweten, had cultural values that systematically encouraged integration with the cycles of life of which they were part. In the case of the Igorots, this meant that farmers who integrated a great number of plants and animals into their gardens were esteemed more than others. This was done by systematically terracing hillsides in a way that created more space for all. Likewise, the Wetsuweten took great care to hunt, fish and harvest in ways that regenerated the animal, river and forest ecosystems with which they interacted. Over the 12,000 years that they presided over the coastal interior of the Pacific Northwest, their ancestors assisted as a barren, post-gacial landscape transformed into a thriving, vital and diverse ecosystem.

Today, as we strive to make our human enterprises green, cyclocentric societies and the coral polyp provide an example for us to follow.

Just as Earth’s processes tended towards diversification, cooperation and resiliency, so too must we intend and achieve with ours. Only when the intention and the result of our process is the support and flourishing of diversity is the principle of diversification met. Only then can our acts and enterprises be considered ecological contributions — and green.

With the fourth earthen ethic in hand, we have another criteria to evaluate and guide our attempts at green: Is our process open to the participation of other species? Does it vitalize the diversification of connected ecologies towards resiliency and cooperation?

Even if the other four Earthen ethics are met, only if a process is supporting and vitalizing a diversity of species in its space can it be considered an ecological contribution.

To see how this Earthen ethic works, let us evaluate the modern endeavours of planting trees.

Often, organizations and individuals will attempt to green a space by planting tree seedling on a barren field. Indeed, by adding one new species to the space (the tree), the biodiversity has increased by one. This is an important step in the green direction. However, if the trees are planted in such a way (a dense grid for example) in which other species are not welcome, and after a period of time the increase of species involvement hasn't not further increased, then, the principle of diversity is not met.

All too often this is the case when the intention of the plantation is not a kincentric support of biodiversity, but rather a humancentric goal of creating a park or of harvesting timber.

To acheive the fourth Earthen ethic, our green enterprises must therefore account for their space and their support for biodiversity.

In his undertaking to understand ancient marine diversity, Sepkoski reviewed thousands of scientific journals by paleontologists and biologists to survey the number of species at the outset of an age and at its end. Only by surveying, naming and listing the species within over a certain period of time and in a certain space, could he demonstrate the process of diversification.

Consequently, our own processes, to be green must likewise demonstrate through a process of naming, listing and accounting, that their space is supportive of diversity. Only by accounting for the net space used by our enterprises (S in cubic meters) and their gradual increase in the net amount of species supported (Nnumber of species) over a period of time (Y years) can we know whether our process, projects and enterprises are in fact supporting biodiversity.

Such a process of accounting, must also involve the naming and knowing the species within our spaces. The naming and knowing of contributors — or not — expresses the depth of a process’s intention to supporting life. And the gradual accumulation of names, demonstrates tangibly the steady welcoming, support and vitalization of biodiversity within our spaces.

In this way, we can intentionally follow the path of Earth, the Iroquois and the Coral Polyp in tending an ever increasing diversity and vitality of life within our shared spaces.

As our life-lists focus our attention to our ecological community, we will find that there were far more creatures at work with us than we ever imagined — species small and mighty enriching the soil and cleaning the air. As we discover creatures that have long been our unnamed friends, their addition become a legitimate boon for our life-lists ratios— and an indication of a phenomenon even more profound unfolding.

For as our garden green spaces transform to forests, and as our forests transform to wild and wondrous sanctums, so too is the consciousness of our connection to it all blossoming like a spring flower. As we will see in our next Earthen Ethic, just as important as diversification is our awareness of our place in the process itself.

This dynamic of matter management can be fund in kincentric cultures and cyclocentric nations around the world.(see our definition of the term cyclocentic in Chapter 5)

As we saw earlier, the lives of the Igorot people of Northern Luzon are guided by their ayyew ecological ethos. This ethos guided the way that the Igorts managed the matter that passed through their hands and bodies. From farming, to eating; from discarding, to defecating, the way in the Igorots embodied ayyew in their process resulted not in "waste" (their is no such concept in the Igorot language) but in "logits" (or, roughly translated "fertilizer").

Each Igorot household home had a lomeng— a submerged enclosure where the family pig was kept. However, these were much more than pig stalls. Lomeng were used to discard everthing from overcooked rice, grass clippings. They were also used as a toilet. The pigs would consume almost everything given to them! They would grow fat and provide rich manure to fertilize the families gardens and field— essentially concentrating the various forms of carbon biomass given to them into even more concentrated and nutrient rich biomass.

Over, countless lifecycles of humans and pigs, this process lead to the steadily concentration of carbon, nitogen and phosphos— the prinpal elements of their ecosystem. This lead to ever more fertile fields and healthy and well nourished Igorot communities.

As we strive to ensure our human enterprises are green, the ways of Igorots, the Ammonites and of the Earth show us the way forward.

Just as the Earth tended it’s cycles towards the inward spiral of matter, so must we intend and achieve the same with our own. Only when the intention and the result of our cycles is the net concentration of their active elements, are we following the pattern of our planet. Only then is the third Earthen ethic met. Only then can our acts and enterprises be considered ecological contributions— and green.

This third earthen ethic provides a geometric means of evaluating the management of matter within our human processes. It applies to the principal elements used in any of our processes— but most notably to our usage of carbon.

Perhaps the most tangible example is that of our modern plastic. As we saw in Plastic's Stellar story, plastic is a by-product of the refinement of petroleum to make fuels. As we send our used plastic off to far off factories to be processed, dumped or incinerated, our industrial processes begin again with new material. Having originated from a concentrated form under the earth to be dispersed over the surface of the planet, we can clearly observe that the outwards spin of plastic industrial cycles is contrary to the inward spiral of the Earth's.

What then are we to make of modern attempts at green— such goals as "net-zero" and "zero-waste"?

In so far as these goals seek to reduce and balance the outputs of our process to zero, they are a step in the right direction. However, from an Earthen perspective we can see that they are fundatmentally insufficient.

As we saw in the example of the Igorots, every molecule of matter in the processes of the Igorots, once its nutrient energy had been extracted, was returned back into the cycles of life around in them in a denser form than before. With the rice plants extracting loose carbon from the air, condensing it in to rice, and Igorot cultivation continuing the downward spiral into further concentration, steadily more and more carbon was sequestered into biomass and into the soil itself.

As we develop enterprises, technologies and industries to be green, the requisite geometry by which they must manage their matter is clear.

Whether the process’s primary element is carbon or calcium, lithium or silicone, water or cement, the process must embody the third Earthen Ethic.

First, the process must accumulate its principal elements. Second, the cycle of the process must concentrate these elements. Third, the spiral of these cycles must then lead to the net-subtraction of the element and tend, over the long-term, to its sequestration.

Circular, for-Earth processes that can achieve this, embody both the inward spiral of their matter and the outward spiral of their energy and fulfill the first three Earthen ethics.

In so doing, the tension between their distribution of energy and their concentration of matter, unfurls through space and time.

Like those cycles of the Igorot, Salmon, Ammonite and the Earth.

In sharp contrast to the desolation that unfolded on the surfaces our fellow planets, the geometry of this Earthen double spiral has led to just the opposite:

A never ending spin towards ever more vibrant and diverse systems.

Our fourth Earthen ethic.
