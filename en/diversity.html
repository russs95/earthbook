“Earth’s spirals of energy and matter tend toward diverse systems.”
― Earthen Ethic No.4

On the floor of oceans around the world, the coral polyp,a tiny marine invertebrate, builds its home. Working a with photosynthetic plankton that lives within its membrane, together both effectively capture carbon and calcium out of the ocean waters, which the polyp then secretes as calcium carbonate to create its protective exoskelonton. Living in colonies of thousands of genetically identical polyps, as generation after generation build upon the other, their handiwork fratally unfold as domes, shelves, horns, fans in a myriad of varying perturbations. The vast surface area and three dimensional space of these coral creations then provide shelter in turn for fish, molusks and other marine creatures of all kinds. The intricate nooks and cranies of the coral reefs and the host of creatures living amongst them, becomes a forge for the flourishing of evolutionary innovations, energizing the diversification of life forms that can dwell in the space. As the coral colonies flourish, so does the marine community, steadily increasing the capture of nutrients, the distribution of energy and the diversification of the ecosystem.





The process by which the coral polyp builds its home, and in so doing creates a common home for a great diversity of others, is an example that must inspire and guide our green aspirations. The way that the polyp lifecycle spirals its matter into sequestration and distributes energy out to its ecosystem, perfectly embodies our first three Earthen ethics. In this confluence of dual spirals, we observe the fourth: an innate support for other species and their diversification. By looking at the way the Earth itself transformed from barren to biodiverse, and by noting the way in which scientists have measured this transition, the demands of this fourth principle shall become clear. Then, with its help we can further make sense of the ecological enrichment wrought by by kincentric and cyclocentric cultures. And, we gain a new tool to evaluate our modern enterprises to be sure they truly are green.

Let us return one more time to Earth's primordial beginnings for a closer look.

As we saw in Earth's stellar story, over the course of the last four billion years, the cosmological character of our planet has unfurled from barren to biosphere. The way in which energy and matter have swirled together has precipitated cyclic systems that have become ever more complex. Part of this complexity has entailed a systematic increase in the net surface area of the organisms that have emerged. From simple algae, to ferns to forests the overall surface area of given area of Earth's surface, has, with life's evolution, exponentially increased the area available for smaller organism to thrive, thus supported and edifying their the overall diversification of life over time.

While we can imagine the way life may have diversified on land and in forests, in fact, it is even more evident in the oceans.

In 1998, paleontologist Joseph Sepkoski completed a comprehensive database of marine life over the last 541 million years. The database compiled over 30,000 fossil genera, representing the finds and categorization of millions of species. By recording each genus’s appearance and disappearance in the fossil timeline, he was able to chart for how long each were active and when they died off.

Sepkoski was particularly interested in showing the Earth’s great rises and falls in diversity — such as the explosion of life in the Carboniferous to the rise and fall of the dinosaurs. Indeed, his timeline of extinctions correlates directly with the dating of great meteors and volcanoes by geologists. However, just as important as showing declines in diversity, his curve rises — demonstrating a phenomenon far more fundamental. Over the history of the Earth the diversity of the biosphere has steadily increased.

This had much to with the conditions on planet Earth (compared to say the surface of Venus or Mars). However, as the process steadily unfolded, it had more and more to do with Earthen systems themselves reflecting and embodying this characteristic.

From ancient carboniferous forests that sheltered the emergent dinausaurs, to coral reefs that sheltered and supported the diversifitication of the oceans, as Earthen ecosystems and biomes have become more abundant, vital and resilient, they have done so through the same inexorable process of diversification. Indeed, this phenomen can be observed in organisms themselves! From mamals to coral polyps within our digestive systems are a whole of host of flourishing microbiomes that reflect the support of diversity on the outside.

Such a support for diversity is also reflected in the ecological integration of cyclocentric cultures.

Around the Great Lakes of North America, the first nation of the Iroquois planted corn along with pumpkins and beans. The cultivation of these three plants together was so complimentary that they were known as the three sisters — providing shade, nitrogen and structure for each other. With sufficient support, the participation of insects, fungi and other plants did not detract from the yield, but added to it. Rather than being closed to diversity, the Iroquois spaces were open and invited participation. Rather than restrictive, it was tended to the benefit of all — corn, pumpkin, bean, humans and anyone else who willing to join the in the fray — thus fully embodying the principal of diversification.

Today, as we strive to make our human enterprises green, the Iroquois, the polyp and Earth’s example shows us the way forward.

Just as Earth’s processes tended towards diversification, cooperation and resiliency, so too must we intend and achieve with ours. Only when the intention and the result of our process is the flourishing of life’s diversity is the principle of diversification met. Only then can our acts and enterprises be considered ecological contributions — and green.

This fourth earthen ethic provides yet another means of evaluating and guiding our human enterprises.

When we evaluate our acts and enterprises we must thus ask: is our process open to the participation of other species? Does it vitalize the diversification of connected ecologies towards resiliency and cooperation?

Only if a process is supporting life’s inherent tendency towards diversity can it be considered an ecological contribution.

Often our modern green endeavours involve planting trees, cultivating fields, tending gardens and even regenerating spaces that have been contaminated or poisoned. While these endeavours are a step in the right direction however, they can fall short of Earth's example. If a process or enterprise is tended first and foremost for the benefit of one species (i.e. humans) in such a way that other species are intentionally excluded, then the fourth Earthen ethic is not met. Here again, we can draw inspiration and guidance from the example of the polyp-- although polyps create a home that is specifically their own space, this space does not preclude a much larger ratio of their created space to be used by any other organism or creature.

To acheive the fourth Earthen ethic, our green enterprises must therefore account for their space and their support for biodiversity.

In his undertaking to understand ancient marine diversity, Sepkoski reviewed thousands of scientific journals by paleontologists and biologists to survey the number of species at the outset of an age and at its end. Only by surveying, naming and listing the species within over a certain period of time and in a certain space, could he demonstrate the process of diversification.

Consequently, our own processes, to be green must likewise demonstrate through a process of naming, listing and accounting, that their space is supportive of diversity. Only by accounting for the net space used by our enterprises (S in cubic meters) and their gradual increase in the net amount of species supported (Nnumber of species) over a period of time (Y years) can we know whether our process, projects and enterprises are in fact supporting biodiversity.

Such a process of accounting, must also involve the naming and knowing the species within our spaces. The naming and knowing of contributors — or not — expresses the depth of a process’s intention to supporting life. And the gradual accumulation of names, demonstrates tangibly the steady welcoming, support and vitalization of biodiversity within our spaces.

In this way, we can intentionally follow the path of Earth, the Igorots and the Coral Polyp in tending an ever increasing diversity and vitality of life within our shared spaces.

However, it is important to note that this third Earthen ethic need not be be the result of physical effort or the energetic restructuring that we see in our first three ethics. As Earth's inexorably multi-billion year process shows us, diversification is geometric consequence of Earth's double spiral of matter and energy tendencies. Thus, by meeting the first three Earthen ethics, our door has been opened to the support of our fellow species. The full force of the Earth’s tendency towards diversity can then express itself and re-vitalize our spaces!

As our life-lists focus our attention to our ecological community, we will find that there were far more creatures at work with us than we ever imagined — species small and mighty enriching the soil and cleaning the air. As we discover creatures that have long been our unnamed friends, their addition become a legitimate boon for our life-lists ratios— and an indication of a phenomenon even more profound unfolding.

For as our garden green spaces transform to forests, and as our forests transform to wild and wondrous sanctums, so too is the consciousness of our connection to it all blossoming like a spring flower. As we will see in our next Earthen Ethic, just as important as diversification is our awareness of our place in the process itself.

Our fifth Earthen ethic.

This dynamic of matter management can be fund in kincentric cultures and cyclocentric nations around the world.(see our definition of the term cyclocentic in Chapter 5)

As we saw earlier, the lives of the Igorot people of Northern Luzon are guided by their ayyew ecological ethos. This ethos guided the way that the Igorts managed the matter that passed through their hands and bodies. From farming, to eating; from discarding, to defecating, the way in the Igorots embodied ayyew in their process resulted not in "waste" (their is no such concept in the Igorot language) but in "logits" (or, roughly translated "fertilizer").

Each Igorot household home had a lomeng— a submerged enclosure where the family pig was kept. However, these were much more than pig stalls. Lomeng were used to discard everthing from overcooked rice, grass clippings. They were also used as a toilet. The pigs would consume almost everything given to them! They would grow fat and provide rich manure to fertilize the families gardens and field— essentially concentrating the various forms of carbon biomass given to them into even more concentrated and nutrient rich biomass.

Over, countless lifecycles of humans and pigs, this process lead to the steadily concentration of carbon, nitogen and phosphos— the prinpal elements of their ecosystem. This lead to ever more fertile fields and healthy and well nourished Igorot communities.

As we strive to ensure our human enterprises are green, the ways of Igorots, the Ammonites and of the Earth show us the way forward.

Just as the Earth tended it’s cycles towards the inward spiral of matter, so must we intend and achieve the same with our own. Only when the intention and the result of our cycles is the net concentration of their active elements, are we following the pattern of our planet. Only then is the third Earthen ethic met. Only then can our acts and enterprises be considered ecological contributions— and green.

This third earthen ethic provides a geometric means of evaluating the management of matter within our human processes. It applies to the principal elements used in any of our processes— but most notably to our usage of carbon.

Perhaps the most tangible example is that of our modern plastic. As we saw in Plastic's Stellar story, plastic is a by-product of the refinement of petroleum to make fuels. As we send our used plastic off to far off factories to be processed, dumped or incinerated, our industrial processes begin again with new material. Having originated from a concentrated form under the earth to be dispersed over the surface of the planet, we can clearly observe that the outwards spin of plastic industrial cycles is contrary to the inward spiral of the Earth's.

What then are we to make of modern attempts at green— such goals as "net-zero" and "zero-waste"?

In so far as these goals seek to reduce and balance the outputs of our process to zero, they are a step in the right direction. However, from an Earthen perspective we can see that they are fundatmentally insufficient.

As we saw in the example of the Igorots, every molecule of matter in the processes of the Igorots, once its nutrient energy had been extracted, was returned back into the cycles of life around in them in a denser form than before. With the rice plants extracting loose carbon from the air, condensing it in to rice, and Igorot cultivation continuing the downward spiral into further concentration, steadily more and more carbon was sequestered into biomass and into the soil itself.

As we develop enterprises, technologies and industries to be green, the requisite geometry by which they must manage their matter is clear.

Whether the process’s primary element is carbon or calcium, lithium or silicone, water or cement, the process must embody the third Earthen Ethic.

First, the process must accumulate its principal elements. Second, the cycle of the process must concentrate these elements. Third, the spiral of these cycles must then lead to the net-subtraction of the element and tend, over the long-term, to its sequestration.

Circular, for-Earth processes that can achieve this, embody both the inward spiral of their matter and the outward spiral of their energy and fulfill the first three Earthen ethics.

In so doing, the tension between their distribution of energy and their concentration of matter, unfurls through space and time.

Like those cycles of the Igorot, Salmon, Ammonite and the Earth.

In sharp contrast to the desolation that unfolded on the surfaces our fellow planets, the geometry of this Earthen double spiral has led to just the opposite:

A never ending spin towards ever more vibrant and diverse systems.

Our fourth Earthen ethic.
