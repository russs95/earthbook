Beneath the mossy floor of North American temperate forests lies an intricate network exchanging the needs and nutrients of its trees. Coursing through the soil, a multitude of fungal species attach to the roots of Spruce, Firs, Poplars and Pines. From one tree to another, these mycorrhizal fungi weave thin threads that interconnect the forest like a fiber-optic web. As the night falls, as the moon ebbs, as the seasons change, the mycelium tapestry allows individual trees to sense the give and take of their networked neighbours. A Birch catching the morning sun through a break in the canopy informs the others by a surge of sugars flowing into its roots. A Spruce short on water lets its neighbours know by drawing more heavily on the network. A Douglas fir attacked by insects alerts its neighbours by a sudden shift in its pattern of give and take. As each individual signals and senses, the collective mutualistically adapts to both cycles and events in their environment. As each individual integrates, so too does it flourish— and with it the collective resiliency, vibrancy and abundance of the forest.





THE WAY THAT a forest and its fungal work together to enrich both the parts and the whole of their ecosystem provides us with our fifth and final Earthen ethic. Already embodying our first four ethics, temperate forests and their mycorrhirzal networks exemplify the enriching spiral of matter and energy tending towards ever more diverse organisms and ever more vibrant, resilient and abundant ecosystems. However, the intricate 'wood wide web' (as scientists affectionately call it), reveals a further, all encompassing phenomenon. As we shall see, the tendency by which the fungal forests steadily increase their awareness is a microcosm of the billion year process by which Earth has transformed from barren to biosphere. By looking closely at how this distinctly Earthen pheonomon has unfolded over our planet's history, we shall lay out our fith and final Earthen ethic. With the help of the principle of 'awaring' (as we will affectionately call it)¹ we can not only ensure that our enterprise are green, we can make them greener and greener. And, most importantly, know that this is so.

To begin, let us return one more time to Earth's primordial story.

A billion years ago, Earth's surface was a barren, desolate place. However, as we saw in Earth's stellar story the materials cycles on its surface adapted to ever better disperse the Sun's shine. Steadily, our planet's unique cosmological character tended its cycles and systems towards ever more effective dissipations of solar energy.¹

As early cellular systems emerged, fundamental to their flourishing was a sensitivity to respond and adapt to changes in their environment. In particular: those that cyclically repeat.²

The first Eukaryotes developed photoreceptors, such as phytochromes and cyptochromes, to detect varying wavelengths and intensities of light.³ As they became aware of the rhythms of night and day, they optimized their bodies and behaviours to capture and transform the energy and matter around them.⁴

As plants emerged, they built upon this primal awareness. Ferns learned to open their stomata during the day and closing them at night to conserve water. Trees learned when in the year to drop their leaves and when to build them. Conifers learned to sync the ebb and flow of their sap with the pull and push of lunar cycles.

As ferns, palms, conifers and others of all kinds proliferate, a second tier of organisms came to survive upon the first. They did so by developing a sensitivity to the oscillations of the green beings whom they depended. Early insects came to sense and sync their reproductive cycle with the blooming cycles of flowering plants. Birds came to sense and sync their movements with to the seasonal fruiting patterns of certain plants. Moths and bats sensed and synced with the cycles of flowers and insects that emerged with the moon.⁵

As organisms fell into webs of independence, the more organisms were aware of the cycles of their fellows, they more they could all adapt. However, this meant more than just sensing their environment, but also signaling to each other. The clearer the signal that its fruit were ripe, the better a tree could share and distribute its seeds. The better a bee could sense a plant's flowers, the more effectively it could find food. The more effectively a fungal thread could find roots seeking nitrogen, them more effectively it could access its sugars and share its nitrogen. As both sense and signal spiraled into countless channels of communication and compensation, the awareness of individuals and of the collective steadily augmented, both above and below the ground.

Here, the temperate forests of today stand as a magnificent Earthen microcosm.

In the same way that Earth's early organisms developed a greater and greater environmental awareness to mutualistically thrive and vitalize Earth's surface, so too do the inhabitants of the mycorrhizal forest mutualistically thrive and vitalize their shared space.

And in the same way that early Earthen systems learned to sense and signal environmental cycles, by signaling and sensing the give and take of their fellow trees, the mycorrhizal forest syncs, adapts and optimizes as a collective. From the yellow of flower to the greenness of a bud, from the fragrance of sap to the form of a fungal fruit, every aspect of the forest informs of ripeness and readiness, health and disease, fertility and death in order that organisms can share resources, warn of threats, and adapt to changing conditions. And as their mutual awareness deepens, so too does their sync with the ebb and flow of the cycles at the base of it all.

As day turns to night, and winter turns to summer, as the moon changes its phase, the entire ecosystem undulates; mutualistically maximizing its collective conversion of solar shine into nutrients for the benefit of all.⁶

The cyclical spiral of awaring of Earth and Forest are examples that we can follow today.

Just like a tree within a forest, our human enterprises give and take from the ecosystems in which they are immersed. As we saw in our second Earthen ethic, our human enterprises are subset systems of the planet's biosphere. The material resources they require, move and transform effect both local ecosystems and the global biosphere. Furthermore the financial energy that our enterprises use is inextricably interconnected with ecological effects. In this way, just as some trees establish a characteristic pattern of give and take, so too do our enterprises.

Today, as we strive to ensure our human enterprises are green, Trees and Earth show us the way forward. Just as Earth tends its processes towards awareness, so too can we can intend with our own. When our enterprises cyclically share their ecological give and take, then the awareness of all is augmented. Such enterprises, by fulfilling this and the other four Earthen ethics, can then be considered to be an ecological contribution, and green.

Conversely, no matter how green an enterprise may seem, if it fails to fully inform how it is green, it fails to be green.

After all...

A product may be marketed as 'green' because it is made with organic material— but a forest may have been cut for the crop.

An organization may operate as a not-for-profit— yet the spaces that it presides over may be barren of biodiversity.

A service may have been made with net-zero emissions— yet tons of carbon may have been dispersed in its shipping.

Or not.

Simply put, without a comprehensive disclosure of all its ecological gives and takes, how can we know the true character of a process?

Whether it is direct concealment or simply a failure of an enterprise to account and know their own impact for themselves, as deleterious as the unshared impact may be, equally so is the failure to have made it known. In so far as the consealment (intentional or not) decreases the ecological awareness of those involved, it directly impedes mutualist adaption: Without accurate knowledge of an enterprises ecological impact, humans seeking to entrain their own cycles and systems to the benefit the biosphere, will make mistakes in their energy allocations: purchasing products, soliciting services or working for enterprises that are taking more than they give.

Thus, when evaluating the greenness of an act, product or process we must ask: Is the enterprise raising the ecological consciousness of all those involved? Has the enterprise undertaken a comprehensive disclosure of all its ecological give and and takes?

In short: is the full ecological character of the enterprise being made known?

Here, character is key.

As we have seen throughout the Tractatus, Earth’s greening of the biosphere was characterized by five fundamental principles. To know an enterprises ecological character, a comprehensive disclosure of an enterprise's ecological give and take must account for each of the Earthen principles. To the extent that an enterprise embodies these five facets of ecological contribution, we can evaluate it shade of green.

1. An enterprise discloses its plan for the cyclical spiral of its products or services in a way that ecologically enrich (defined by the numerical balances of facets 2, 3, 4).

2. An enterprise discloses its spiral of financial energy out to the benefit of people and planet.

3. An enterprise discloses the spiral of its principal elements (such as carbon) towards concentration and sequestration.

4. An enterprise discloses the spiral of its spaces towards the support of increasing biodiversity.

5. An enterprises expresses and realizes its commitment of cyclical disclosure (i.e. daily, quarterly, annually, etc.) of facets 1-4.

Through such a five faceted ecological accounting and disclosure, the color of our enterprises, products and processes will scintillate like a tree’s Spring buds — a vivid green a beacon to eyes weary of a long grey winter.

A sandal maker can declare its plan to re-use automobile tire for its soles, then recover the rubber at the shoe's end and sequester them — and proudly report at the end of the year the results.

A web-server provider can declare its for-Earth business structure— and proudly report each year the results of their annual impact sequestering carbon and supporting biodiversity in their spaces.

A swim-wear maker can proudly declare its for-Earth business priority— along with its plan for the sequestration of its polyesters and its net-carbon subtraction for the year.

A dress maker can proudly declare how its organic fabric is biodegradable — and also the biodiversity its space has supported that year.

A book author can declare their intention to support raise ecological consciousness — and share the impact their enterprise and space at the end of each year.

With renewed confidence, we can then choose to work for or buy from such enterprises, sure of the color, character and the well-being vitalized. With our support, such enterprises can steadily increase their give to vastly exceed their take — for their well being, ours and everyone’s.

This full embodiment of the five principles of Earthen ethics is what we shall call a for-Earth process of human enterprise.

Powered by humanity's rising planet passion such Earth Enterprises are uniquely poised to transform even the most dire systemic pollutions from spirals of depletion into spirals of enrichment.

Starting with our modern management of plastic.
