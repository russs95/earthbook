“Ayka ta man gagayam ta"
(Come, let’s tell some stories)
― Igorot expression
In 2011, the sheer amount of dirty diapers, sandals, bags and wrappers entering the Chico River had reached a breaking point. Downriver, overwhelmed by the ever arriving flow of plastic, the various municipalities of the Filipino province of Kalinga took action to sue, the upriver town of Bontoc. The mayor Bontoc, took the charges seriously, and strove to shut down the various legal and illegal dumpsites that were creating the problem.

But where was all our plastic to go?

For those of us living along the river, the challenge was palpable. For centuries the Igorot people of this mountainous region had lived in fierce and independence from the rest of the country. However, over the last decades, the flow of modern of modern goods-- in particular modern foods-- had begun to make its way into the remote towns and villages of the region. Whereas before, the leaves and weaving that covered food and bodies was entirely biodegradeable, now strange modern materials refused to degrade when disgarded.

However, for the last decade all sorts of upcycling innovations had emerged to deal with the various materials. Guided by the Igorot virtue of Ayyew, folks around the region strove to put the materials to richer use. In the mines plastic dynamic cable was being used instead of rattan to weave bags. Plastic straws were being used to weave mats. Silver wrappers were being used to make sachels and bags of all kinds. 

It was a movement that both myself and Irene were involved in, and fascinated by. Wheareas Banayan had been working on eduction platforms, I had been working on upcycled innovations-- both of us with limited success. Despite our tremendously different backgrounds, Banayan and I immediately hit it off.  

As the legal stand-off in Bontoc intensified, we realized that securing plastic out of the environment was far more vital than making  handicrafts. However, my colleagues and I in the village of Sabangan had recently been experimenting with a technique from South America in which PET bottles were up-cycled into bricks, by simply packing them full of sand. It just so happened that the river sand bar, and the local dump site were side by side. My friends and I decided to try packing our bottles full of plastic instead of sand.
